Meta-Analysis Protocol: Coral Wound Healing &amp; Tissue RegenerationA Beginner&apos;s Guide to Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis0. INTRODUCTION TO META-ANALYSISMeta-analysis is a statistical method that combines data from multiple independent studies to identify patterns, estimate overall effect sizes, and detect sources of variability. It is particularly useful in ecology for resolving conflicting findings and revealing generalities across diverse systems.What this meta-analysis will accomplish:Quantify average coral healing rates across species, environments, and wound typesUnderstand how coral traits (taxonomy, morphology, tissue type) shape regeneration outcomesTest specific hypotheses about environmental stressors and healing successIdentify knowledge gaps to guide future research prioritiesCreate an open-access database of coral healing metrics and metadataWhy PRISMA guidelines?We follow PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) guidelines to ensure transparency and reproducibility. This means documenting every step so someone else could exactly reproduce our search, data collection, analysis, and figures.1. OBJECTIVES &amp; SCOPEPrimary Goal: To systematically review and meta-analyze coral regeneration after wounding, focusing on healing rates, coral traits, environmental influences, wound types, and geographic patterns.Taxonomic Scope: Stony corals (Scleractinia) only. We explicitly exclude octocorals and other analog taxa.Key Deliverables:Quantitative summary of coral wound healing across studiesIdentification of moderators of healing (species traits, environment, wound type)Phylogenetic and morphological signals of regeneration capacityOpen-access database of healing metrics and metadata for the community2. RESEARCH QUESTIONS &amp; HYPOTHESESPrimary Research QuestionsWhat is the average rate and extent of coral wound healing?How do coral traits (species, morphology, perforate/imperforate) influence regeneration?What is the effect of wound type and geometry (e.g., perimeter-to-area ratio)?How do environmental variables (temperature, nutrients, pH, sedimentation) moderate healing?Are there biogeographic patterns in regeneration capacity?Key Hypotheses to TestTrait-based hypothesis: Imperforate corals regenerate faster than perforate coralsTemperature hypothesis: Elevated temperatures near bleaching thresholds reduce regeneration successNutrient hypothesis: Chronic low-level nutrient enrichment may enhance regeneration under some conditionsWound type hypothesis: Grazing-type wounds result in faster tissue regrowth than structural injuriesGeometry hypothesis: Lesion geometry (perimeter-to-area ratio) predicts regeneration rate, with higher P/A leading to faster closureColony integration hypothesis: Resource translocation from healthy tissue supports healing of larger wounds3. INCLUSION &amp; EXCLUSION CRITERIAInclude:Empirical studies on coral wound healing and tissue regenerationExperimental or observational studies (lab, field, or mesocosm)Studies measuring healing rates, tissue regeneration, or regrowth metricsStudies providing species-level identificationExclude:Reviews, editorials, and conference abstracts without full dataStudies focused only on disease progression without regeneration dataNon-stony coral species (e.g., octocorals, hydrozoans, anemones)Modeling studies lacking empirical healing rate dataStudies without identifiable wound type or coral species ID5. EFFECT SIZE CALCULATION &amp; VARIANCETo synthesize healing and related ecological responses, we define effect-size formulas with clear units. When studies lack raw variances, we will standardize effect sizes in metafor using robust variance estimation.6.1 Primary Healing MetricsHierarchy of metrics (preferred → fallback):Rate constant k: Exponential decay rate from temporal area data (day⁻¹) — PREFERREDLinear/areal rates: (D_initial - D_final) / Δt or (A_initial - A_final) / ΔtProportional rate: (% closure) / Δt when only two time points availableTime to closure: 1 / (days to 100% heal) — can approximate a rateImportant considerations:Convert all measurements to standard units: mm or mm² for size, days for timeExtract raw temporal data when available so we can fit models flexiblyFlag the method used for each study so we can test for method effectsDecide in analysis whether to meta-analyze rates directly or use standardized metrics6.2 Secondary OutcomesSome studies report fitness-related effects of wounding:Growth rate: Change in colony area or linear extension (cm/day or cm²/day)Fecundity: Larvae or eggs per colony or per polypSurvival: Proportion alive at end of study (can use odds ratio for wounded vs control)Standardization approach:Log Response Ratio (lnRR) for continuous variables:lnRR = ln(mean_injured / mean_control)Odds Ratio for binary outcomes (alive/dead, infected/clean)6.3 Variance Extraction &amp; ImputationRequired for each effect size:Mean or rate valueMeasure of dispersion (SD, SE, or CI)Sample size (n)When dispersion is missing:Back-calculate SE from confidence intervals or p-values when possibleEstimate SD using rule of thumb: range ÷ 4 or IQR ÷ 1.35Impute SD via mean-SD regression across compiled dataApply robust variance estimation (RVE) in metafor for nested or sparse dataEach record will note its variance method: &quot;Reported&quot; | &quot;Back-calculated&quot; | &quot;Imputed&quot;7. STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN7.1 Descriptive AnalysisSummarize studies by taxa, wound type, study location, and methodologyCreate forest plots showing healing rates across studiesIdentify most studied species (Porites, Acropora, Favia, Pocillopora) and knowledge gaps7.2 Meta-Analytic ModelsGrand mean healing rate: Random-effects model with rma.mv() in metaforHeterogeneity tests: Calculate I² and Q statistics to assess variabilityMeta-regression: Test moderators (species, wound size, P/A ratio, temperature, etc.)Publication bias: Funnel plots and Egger&apos;s test7.3 Advanced AnalysesPhylogenetic meta-analysis: If we can assemble a coral phylogeny + trait databaseInteraction effects: Test temperature × tissue type, wound size × colony size, etc.Temporal trajectory models: Fit exponential models to raw healing time-series when available8. KEY INSIGHTS FROM ELICIT REVIEWThe Elicit AI review analyzed 40 highly relevant studies and identified several important patterns that should inform your meta-analysis:9.1 Cellular &amp; Molecular MechanismsHealing phases: Plug formation → immune cell infiltration → granulation → tissue maturation → symbiont reestablishmentImmune activation: Toll-like receptor pathways, phenoloxidase activity, melanin synthesis (6 studies)NEW finding (2024): Stem-like cells identified at wound margins with proliferative capacity (Levanoni et al.)Resource transport: 14C labeling shows photosynthate translocation to injury sites, especially for large lesions (4 studies)9.2 Factors Influencing HealingLesion characteristics: Small lesions heal faster; P/A ratio predicts healing rateColony size &amp; depth: Larger, shallower colonies generally heal fasterEnvironmental stressors: Elevated temperature, ocean acidification, sedimentation, and turbidity negatively affect healingNutrient effects: Some evidence that ammonium enrichment may mitigate heat stress effectsLesion spacing: Closely spaced wounds heal more slowly (resource competition)9.3 Species PatternsMost studied genera: Porites (14), Acropora (9), Pocillopora (8), Favia (7), Montastraea/Orbicella (8)Interspecific variation: Substantial differences in healing strategies (marginal vs new polyps vs stem cells)Morphology: Branching and massive corals generally have greater regenerative ability than encrusting formsFast healers: Acropora palmata, Porites astreoides; Slow healers: Agaricia agaricites9.4 Methodological InsightsStudy design: 27 field, 19 lab, 4 both — mix helps with generalizability vs. mechanismCommon techniques: Imaging/histology (14), molecular methods (8), 14C labeling (4), microbiome (2)Wound creation: Airbrushing, Waterpik, bone cutters, air jets — method may affect healing trajectoryKey measurements: 35/40 studies measured healing rate or time; 8 measured lesion characteristics10.
