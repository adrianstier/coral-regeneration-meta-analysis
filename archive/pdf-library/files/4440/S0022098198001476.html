Depth-related variation in regeneration of artificial lesions in the Caribbean corals Porites astreoides and Stephanocoenia michelinii

Author links open overlay panelI Nagelkerken a, E.H Meesters b, R.P.M Bak b c
https://doi.org/10.1016/S0022-0981(98)00147-6Get rights and content
Full text access

Abstract

Regeneration of artificial lesions was studied along a depth gradient from 5–35 m in the Caribbean corals Porites astreoides (brown morph) and Stephanocoenia michelinii (massive morph). During the first 3–4 weeks of the regeneration process tissue recovery in both coral species was higher in shallow water than in deep water colonies. After 7 weeks when the lesions were almost closed, however, shallow as well as deep water colonies had restored the damage to the same degree, showing that regeneration is a high priority process in coral ecology. The deep water colonies did not show signs of photoacclimatization. The zooxanthellae densities and the total chlorophyll concentration per algal cell in the coral tissue did not vary significantly with depth. Furthermore, there was no decrease with depth of the chlorophyll a/c2 ratio, which would have indicated photoacclimatization by means of a relative increase of chlorophyll c2 which is more efficient in using the blue light available at depth. One possibility which may explain the slower tissue recovery in deep water colonies during the first stage of the regeneration process, is a combination of the reduced light levels at depth and a lack of chlorophyll acclimatization in the deep water colonies.

1. Introduction

Regeneration is an important life history strategy in corals (Bak, 1983, Meesters et al., 1992, Meesters and Bak, 1995, Meesters et al., 1996). Corals can be damaged as a result of various biotic and abiotic causes (see review by Pearson, 1981) and with failing of regeneration, lesions will become a permanent feature on the colony surface (Meesters et al., 1996, Meesters et al., 1997b).
Regeneration has been studied in relation to colony size (Loya, 1976), spatial competition (Bak et al., 1977), lesion size and shape (Bak and Steward Van-Es, 1980, Meesters et al., 1997a, Oren et al., 1997), lesion position on the colony (Meesters et al., 1992, Meesters and Bak, 1995), environmental factors (Lester and Bak, 1985), sedimentation (Meesters et al., 1992), coral bleaching (Meesters and Bak, 1993), fecundity (Van Veghel and Bak, 1994), growth (Bak, 1983, Meesters et al., 1994), and as an ecophysiological trait in coral morphs (Nagelkerken and Bak, 1998). However, all these studies were largely limited to the reef crest, the reef terrace or the shallow reef slope. Various coral species are distributed along a relatively large depth range, but little is known of the regeneration of coral colonies in deeper water.
Corals often have limited energy budgets, and energy is partitioned over important processes such as growth, reproduction and regeneration (Bak, 1983, Edmunds and Spencer Davies, 1986, Spencer Davies, 1991). Regeneration is fuelled by the polyps surrounding and in the vicinity of the lesion and requires a considerable amount of energy (Meesters et al., 1994, Meesters et al., 1997a, Oren et al., 1997). In spite of the high energetic cost, a trade-off in energy exists in favor of regeneration and to the detriment of growth (Bak, 1983, Meesters et al., 1994) and fecundity (Van Veghel and Bak, 1994), which indicates the importance of this process.
Light is of primary importance for the survival of reef-building corals (Chalker et al., 1988). Photosynthetically fixed carbon is translocated from the symbiotic algae to the coral host and serves as a source of energy (Patton et al., 1977, Crossland et al., 1980, Muscatine et al., 1984, Stimson, 1987). Light intensity decreases with depth (e.g. Bosscher and Meesters, 1992) and corals living at greater depths may have less energy for regeneration and other processes. Several studies have already shown that corals growing in deeper waters and/or at reduced light levels show a decrease in other energy-requiring physiological functions, such as growth rate (Dustan, 1975, Hubbard and Scaturo, 1985, Huston, 1985, Bosscher and Meesters, 1992), fecundity (Kojis and Quinn, 1984) and respiration rate (Spencer Davies, 1977, Spencer Davies, 1980, Chalker et al., 1983).
Our hypothesis is that as a result of the decreasing light intensity at increasing depth corals have less energy reserves available for the regeneration process, resulting in a lower regeneration rate in corals growing at greater depths. To test this hypothesis, the regeneration of artificial lesion was studied in two Caribbean coral species along a depth range from 5–35 m. To account for the effect of photoacclimatization—the process as a result of which corals can acclimatize to lower light levels trough, for example, a change in the photosynthetic pigment content and organization—we measured densities of zooxanthellae and concentrations of chlorophyll a and chlorophyll c2 per algal cell.

2. Materials and methods

The scleractinian corals Porites astreoides (Lamarck) and Stephanocoenia michelinii (Milne-Edwards and Haime) were selected to study the regeneration of artificial lesions in relation to water depth. Both coral species have a relatively flat colony surface with small polyps, facilitating infliction of artificial lesions, and resulting in an evenly distributed light regime over the colony's surface.
The study site was located at the Seaquarium reef on the leeward coast of Curaçao, Netherlands Antilles. A detailed description of the reef structure and distribution of corals on the reefs of Curaçao is given by Bak, 1975, Bak, 1977.
Along a depth-gradient from 5 to 35 m, 41 colonies of Porites astreoides and 29 colonies of Stephanocoenia michelinii were haphazardly selected. These two coral species both consist of at least two morphs, which show a different rate of regeneration (Nagelkerken and Bak, 1998). In this study we used the brown morph of P. astreoides and the massive morph of S. michelinii (see Nagelkerken and Bak, 1998). Cylindrical lesions of approximately 1 cm in diameter and 2–3 mm deep were inflicted in situ on top of the experimental colonies, during January 1993. The lesions were made with a small grinding stone attached to a pneumatic drill powered by a SCUBA tank. Mean lesion size (±S.D.) after lesion infliction was 2.2±0.3 cm2 for P. astreoides and 2.0±0.6 cm2 for S. michelinii. Recovery of lesions was assessed after approximately 4, 7, 15, 21, 29, 36 and 49 days following lesion infliction and was measured as described by Meesters and Bak (1993).
All experimental colonies were collected at the end of the regeneration experiment. Using a drilling machine with a hollow drill, cores of the skeleton plus tissue were drilled adjacent to the lesions. The cores had a diameter of 2.5 cm for Porites astreoides and 1.2 cm for Stephanocoenia michelinii. The live tissue was removed from the cores and zooxanthellae densities and chlorophyll a and chlorophyll c2 concentrations were determined as in Szmant and Gassman (1990).
Recovery of lesions was expressed as the percentage of the initial lesion area closed. Regression slopes (percentage lesion area closed versus depth) were calculated at each time interval using least-squares linear regression (Sokal and Rohlf, 1995). Regression slopes were compared using the T′-method and Gabriel's approximate method (Sokal and Rohlf, 1995).

3. Results

3.1. Regeneration

The data show that regeneration is influenced by depth in both coral species (Fig. 1, 2). When lesion recovery is depicted versus time for the 10 shallowest (5–10 m for Porites astreoides, 5–16 m for Stephanocoenia michelinii) and 10 deepest (24–34 m for P. astreoides, 21–30 m for S. michelinii) experimental colonies, a consistent pattern is visible (Fig. 1a,b). During the first 3–4 weeks more tissue was regenerated in lesions on shallow water colonies than on deep ones, whereas after that time the recovery was more or less similar.
In shallow water colonies of both coral species an average of about 40% of the lesion area was recovered in just 4 days, compared to 20% in the deep water Porites astreoides and only 5% in the deep water Stephanocoenia michelinii (Fig. 1a,b). In both species, shallow and deep water colonies had regenerated an average of approximately 90% of the total lesion area after 49 days, and most lesions were not completely closed at the end of the experiment. Considering all colonies along the entire depth range, only 15% of all lesions in P. astreoides and 10% in S. michelinii were closed after 49 days.
The regression slopes of the regeneration curves (i.e. percent lesion area recovered vs depth) were initially negative in Porites astreoides and Stephanocoenia michelinii, but later approached zero and ultimately became slightly positive (Fig. 2). The largest difference with depth (i.e. steepest slope) at any time was observed at day 4 in both species (Fig. 2). The relatively strong negative slope indicates that recovery is much higher in shallower water colonies than in deep water colonies. The slope in both species gradually became less steep and as of day 29 in P. astreoides and day 21 in S. michelinii it became zero (Fig. 2), indicating there was no difference any longer in the lesion recovery between shallow water and deep water colonies. Afterwards, the regression slopes became slightly positive in both species, indicating that recovery was slightly higher in deep water colonies compared to the shallow ones (Fig. 2). The slope at day 4 was significantly lower than the slope at day 36 in P. astreoides, while in S. michelinii the slope at day 4 was significantly lower than the slope at day 21, 30, 37 and 49 (Fig. 2).
The pattern of regeneration versus depth differed somewhat between Porites astreoides and Stephanocoenia michelinii. At days 4, 7 and 15, the regression slope was steeper in S. michelinii than in P. astreoides (Fig. 2), indicating a larger difference in recovery between shallow and deep water colonies in S. michelinii during the first 15 days of the regeneration process.

3.2. Zooxanthellae

Densities of zooxanthellae did not differ significantly with depth (P>0.209) in Porites astreoides or in Stephanocoenia michelinii (Fig. 3).

3.3. Chlorophyll

The total chlorophyll concentration (i.e. chl. a+c2) per algal cell did not show any significant differences with depth (P>0.090) in Stephanocoenia michelinii or in Porites astreoides (Fig. 4). For the chlorophyll a/c2 ratio a significant increase with depth (P=0.028) was found in P. astreoides (Fig. 5), although only a relatively small part of the relation could be explained by the factor depth (r2=0.12). In S. michelinii, the chlorophyll a/c2 ratio did not show a significant variation with depth (P=0.510).

4. Discussion

The data in the present study show that regeneration varies with depth in both coral species. The recovery of artificial lesions in Porites astreoides and Stephanocoenia michelinii is negatively related with water depth, but only during the initial phase of the regeneration process. When the lesions are almost closed, however, the mean percentage of recovered lesion area is similar for shallow and deep water colonies. Bak (1983)studied the recovery of lesions in P. astreoides colonies at 10 and 23 m depth and found the percentage of recovered lesions to be higher in shallow water colonies. This also suggests an influence of depth on coral regeneration, but the results cannot directly be related to the present data due to difference in lesion characteristics.
Colonies of Porites astreoides and Stephanocoenia michelinii did not show signs of photoacclimatization with depth by means of an increase of the total chlorophyll concentration per cell or a variation in the zooxanthellae density. It is known that various coral species are able to photoacclimatize when living at greater depths (Chalker et al., 1988). As a response to the reduced light levels deep water corals often have increased levels of pigments in their tissue (Porter et al., 1984, Battey and Porter, 1988). A further indication of lack of photoacclimatization in P. astreoides and S. michelinii is the lack of a decrease in the chlorophyll a/c2 ratio with depth. Chlorophyll c2 is much more efficient in absorbing the blue light available at greater depths. A decrease in the chlorophyll a/c2 ratio with depth would therefore indicate a relative increase in the chlorophyll c2 concentration compared to that of chlorophyll a, allowing for a more efficient use of the light available at depth (Battey and Porter, 1988).
The lack of chlorophyll acclimatization in combination with reduced light levels at increasing depth may be the responsible factors which could explain the faster response of tissue recovery in shallow water corals compared to deep water corals in Porites astreoides and Stephanocoenia michelinii. Regeneration of tissue requires, apart from nitrogen acquired through heterotrophic feeding, a considerable amount of energy which must be supplied by the polyps surrounding and in the vicinity of the lesion (Meesters et al., 1994, Meesters et al., 1997a, Oren et al., 1997). In shallow water, corals are often metabolically supersaturated with light resulting in a reserve of excess energy (Muscatine et al., 1984, Spencer Davies, 1984, Edmunds and Spencer Davies, 1986), while lipid production has also been shown to be light-dependent (Patton et al., 1977Crossland et al., 1980Battey and Porter, 1988). Consequently, deep water corals receiving lower light levels may be restricted in their energy reserves. Porter et al. (1984)calculated that shade-acclimatized colonies of Stylophora pistillata acquire less than half of their metabolic carbon through photosynthesis, while Stimson (1987)showed that corals kept at experimentally low light levels had reduced lipid levels. Hence, one possibility which may explain the faster response of tissue recovery in shallow water corals may be the presence of more extensive energy reserves, in comparison to deep water corals. Further work on the carbon and nitrogen budgets of P. astreoides and S. michelinii should indicate whether this hypothesis is correct. In Stephanocoenia michelinii, larger energy reserves in shallow water corals are probably not acquired by means of an increase in fatty acids, as these have been found to be relatively constant along a depth gradient of 3–24 m (Meyers et al., 1978). An alternative hypothesis is that the deep water colonies harbor different species of zooxanthellae (Rowan and Knowlton, 1995, Rowan et al., 1997) than the shallow water colonies, and have a different rate of photosynthesis or translocation of photosynthetic energy to the coral.
The fact that ultimately the degree of lesion area which has been recovered is similar in shallow water and deep water colonies (Fig. 1), shows that regeneration is an important process in coral ecology (Bak, 1983, Meesters et al., 1994). In spite of the fact that deep water colonies live at lower light levels and did not show signs of photoacclimatization, shallow as well as deep water colonies of Porites astreoides and Stephanocoenia michelinii had both regenerated an average lesion area of about 90% after 49 days. As the recovery was faster in shallow water colonies during the initial phase of lesion recovery, it is apparent that the regeneration rate must have been higher in the deep water colonies during the last phase of the recovery in order to ultimately result in the same degree of tissue recovery.
Two of the possibilities which may explain the origin of the energy required for faster recovery in deep water corals during the last phase of the regeneration process are: (1) deep water colonies of P. astreoides and S. michelinii may be more dependent on additional sources of energy through heterotrophic feeding. Various studies have already suggested that deep water colonies of various coral species, including P. astreoides, are dependent on allochthonous sources of carbon (Meyers, 1979, Wellington, 1982, Muscatine et al., 1989). (2) Deep water colonies of P. astreoides and S. michelinii may be more dependent on translocation of energy for regeneration from other physiological processes. Energy can be translocated to the regeneration site to the detriment of other physiological processes. For example, coral growth is negatively affected in regenerating corals, even in water as shallow as 1–2 m (Bak, 1983) and 7 m (Meesters et al., 1994), while the fecundity and number of gonads per polyp are reduced near regenerating lesions of corals at a depth of 11–16 m (Van Veghel and Bak, 1994).
